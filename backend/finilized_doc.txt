teaching, learning, and research within higher education institutions. They serve as central access points for academic resources, enabling knowledge sharing and scholarly development. The effectiveness of library services largely depends on how efficiently materials are cataloged, accessed, and circulated. As universities expand in size and digital engagement, traditional manual approaches to managing library resources have become increasingly insufficient.At Hawassa University, essential library functions such as cataloging, borrowing, returning, reservation, and in-library circulation are predominantly handled using manual procedures and spreadsheet-based (XLS) records. These methods require considerable human effort and are prone to errors, leading to data inconsistency, delayed service delivery, and challenges in maintaining accurate records.Hawassa University operates across seven campuses, each with one or more libraries, creating a hierarchical and distributed library structure. Managing physical and digital materials across these campuses using manual systems presents significant challenges in maintaining centralized records, enforcing consistent access policies, and coordinating resource utilization. A distinctive feature of the university’s libraries is the use of approval-based policies to regulate borrowing privileges, which ensures academic accountability. However, the reliance on manual procedures and informal record-keeping further increases administrative workload and complicates coordination among departments and library staff.Furthermore, the growing demand for digital materials such as e-books, academic journals, theses, and research papers highlights the limitations of manual systems, which are unable to enforce access control, track usage, or provide secure and efficient access. While this approach has been functional for small-scale operations, it is now inadequate for a large and expanding university community. Research indicates that manual library systems are prone to data inaccuracies, difficulties in tracking materials, slow service delivery, and a high risk of record loss HYPERLINK \l "gu"[3].The lack of a digital platform forces students to physically visit the library to check book availability, resulting in long queues and congestion during peak hours. Similar challenges are observed in many Ethiopian academic libraries, where staff spend considerable time on repetitive manual tasks instead of focusing on service improvement and Students also report dissatisfaction with delays and limited access [4]. Overall, the existing manual system reduces operational efficiency, limits accessibility to academic resources, and hinders effective resource management.Within the proposed system, the following specific challenges will be addressed:Inefficient and Error-Prone Circulation: The card-based system requires physical handling of user and material cards for every circulation, causing slow service, human errors, and difficulty in returning students’ ID, returns, and overdue items.Ineffective Borrowing, Reservation, and Account Management: Borrowing, reservation, and related account activities such as checking overdue items, fines, and borrowing eligibility are handled manually. Manual Approval Process: Department heads manually record approved students on paper and submit the list to library staff. This manual process is slow, prone to errors, lacks transparency, and increases the workload of both department heads and library staff.Lack of Centralized, Hierarchical Management: The absence of a unified digital platform prevents cohesive resource management and sharing across campuses, limiting policy enforcement and accurate, consolidated reporting.Poor Accessibility and User Experience: Students and faculty must be physically present to check book availability. There is no mechanism for online reservations, renewals, or secure access to digital collections, which causes delays and limits visibility of available materialsThese challenges demonstrate the critical need for a Digital Library Reservation and Book Borrowing System at Hawassa University. Such a system will automate core library processes, enhance data accuracy, improve accessibility, streamline approval workflows, and enable effective hierarchical management across multiple campuses, ultimately improving the overall quality and efficiency of library services.1.3 Objectives of the Project1.3.1 General ObjectiveThe general objective of this project is to design and implement a web-based Digital Library Reservation and Book Borrowing System for Hawassa University.1.3.2 Specific objectivesTo achieve the general objective, the specific objectives of the project are:To conduct comprehensive requirement gathering and analysis.To design the overall system architecture.To implement the front-end and back-end components of the system.To test the system using appropriate testing methodologies.To deploy the system and perform monitoring in a real environment.1.4 Scope of the StudyThe scope of the proposed system focuses on improving the management and accessibility of library resources at Hawassa University through a user-friendly web-based platform. The system streamlines essential library processes, including searching for materials, reserving books, borrowing materials for use outside the library, and using materials on-site (library-only circulation). Advanced search filters help users locate resources efficiently, reducing the need for traditional manual record-keeping and coordination.The platform manages payments and fines for overdue, lost, or damaged materials, ensuring proper accountability and enforcement of library policies. Users are expected to have legitimate access to borrow, reserve, or use library resources, and borrowing privileges are granted only after approval by their department head. The system automatically sends notifications and reminders regarding due dates, overdue items, and reservation status, helping users comply with library policies efficiently.The system supports hierarchical roles to manage library operations effectively. A super admin oversees the overall system and creates accounts for library admins. Each library admin oversees the daily operations of their library, managing users, and library resources with the support of the system. By centralizing these operations and automating critical tasks, the system enhances operational efficiency, ensures accurate record-keeping, and provides a secure, seamless, and accessible experience for all stakeholders.1.5 Limitation of the StudyWhile the proposed system offer significant potential for improving library services, it is essential to recognize certain limitations that may impact the system's implementation:Limitation on user coverageThe system is designed primarily for Hawassa University campuses and does not provide services for external users, such as visiting researchers or other institutions. This limits the system’s reach beyond the university community.Limitation on integration with other university systemsIntegration with central university systems is limited due to data sensitivity and privacy policies, which restrict access to certain student and staff information. As a result, operations such as real-time synchronization with other databases may not be fully automated.Limitation on system accessibility and operationsAll system features require an active internet connection to function properly. Any interruption in connectivity can affect access to system service, potentially impacting the overall user experienceLimited accessibility for visually impaired usersThe system may not fully support screen readers or keyboard navigation, making it difficult for blind users to access resources and use system features independently.1.6 Methodology1.6.1 Data Collection MethodologyTo understand the existing library operations at Hawassa University and identify requirements for the proposed System, two data collection methods were used:InterviewsSemi-structured interviews were conducted with librarians, academic staff, and students. These interviews explored how borrowing is currently handled, challenges encountered, and user expectations for a digital system.ObservationDirect observation of daily library activities provided insight into how books are recorded, how returns are managed, and the limitations of the manual workflow. This helped validate the information gathered from interviews.1.6.2 System Analysis and Design MethodologyThe proposed system follows a structured system analysis and design methodology to transform collected requirements into a clear and reliable system blueprint. This approach ensures that each development activity is performed in a logical sequence, providing a strong foundation for the subsequent phases of the project.Requirements AnalysisIn this phase, both functional and non-functional requirements are identified and documented based on stakeholder needs. Key stakeholders include library staff, system developers, and end-users such as students and faculty members. The outcome of this phase is a well-defined requirements specification that clearly describes system behavior, performance expectations, and security considerations.System ModelingOnce the requirements are established, system modeling is carried out to visualize system functionality and data flow. This includes the development of use case diagrams, entity-relationship (ER) diagrams, activity diagrams, sequence diagrams, and process flow diagrams. These models help in understanding system interactions, workflows, and relationships between system components.Design ApproachThe design phase applies object-oriented and structured design principles to ensure the system is modular, scalable, and easy to maintain. This phase focuses on defining system components, data structures, and interactions while maintaining clarity and consistency across the overall system design.1.6.3 System ImplementationSystem implementation, also known as coding, is the process of converting the functional requirements identified during the system analysis phase and the detailed specifications from the design phase into executable programs. The main objective of this phase is to develop a fully functional system that meets business requirements, ensures data integrity, and provides a secure, reliable, and user-friendly interface.Development ApproachThis project adopts an Agile Software Development Approach, specifically an iterative and incremental model. Instead of following a rigid linear sequence, Agile allows the team to develop the system in small, manageable increments (sprints), where each sprint includes planning, design, implementation, and testing activities.Justification for Using Agile MethodologyAgile methodology is selected for this project because:Requirements evolve during interviews and observationIn this study, user requirements come from students, librarians, and academic staff. Their needs may change as they interact with early prototypes. Agile methodology supports such evolving requirements by allowing continuous refinement during each sprint.Frequent feedback leads to a more user-friendly systemThe proposed system must match user expectations in terms of usability. Agile methodology enables the team to deliver early versions of core features search, reservation, borrowing so stakeholders can test them and provide immediate feedback.Reduces risk of system misalignmentTraditional models often lead to a final system that differs from what users imagined. Agile methodology minimizes this risk through ongoing demonstrations, reviews, and adjustments throughout development.Implementation Tools and TechnologiesThe will be implemented as a web-based application using the following tools and technologies:1. Core operations such as registration, cataloging, borrowing, returning, reservation, and record-keeping are handled manually, with limited use of Excel for inventory management. Library materials including textbooks, journals, reference books, and digital resources are physically cataloged and monitored.Borrowing and reservation processes require students to present their ID cards, which are attached to material records, increasing the risk of loss or misplacement. Users must visit the library in person to search for materials or check availability, often leading to long queues during peak hours. Additionally, some materials with limited copies are restricted to in-library use only, reducing accessibility and efficiency.The current system relies on multiple staff roles, with front desk staff managing in-library use, stack staff handling borrowing and returning, and technical staff maintaining the catalog. Coordination between campuses is performed manually, causing delays in material transfers and communication. Reporting is limited to basic summaries, lacking real-time data and detailed analytics.Although the system supports essential library functions, it suffers from inefficiencies such as slow transactions, poor material tracking, lack of real-time availability, and reliance on manual processes. The absence of overdue fine enforcement further reduces effectiveness. These limitations highlight the need for a Digital Library Reservation and Book Borrowing System to improve efficiency, accessibility, and resource management across all campuses.2.2 Proposed System DescriptionThe proposed system is a comprehensive web-based platform designed to modernize library operations at Hawassa University across all campuses. The system integrates traditional library services with digital management tools to create an efficient, accessible, and user-friendly environment for students, lecturers, and library staff. By automating core functions such as borrowing, returning, reservation, and circulation of materials, the platform aims to enhance the overall user experience, improve resource tracking, and support administrative decision-making.Key Features:Library Collection and Discovery: The system provides a unified catalog for both digital and physical resources, allowing users to browse and search for materials using various filters, such as material type, subject, or author. This feature ensures that users can locate the resources they need efficiently.Borrowing and Returning: Users can borrow available materials by checking the available materials through the system. Physical materials intended for in-library use are also tracked and managed digitally to ensure proper circulation. The system records all transactions to maintain accurate and reliable borrowing history.Reservation System: Users can reserve unavailable materials directly through the system. Materials that are currently borrowed can be reserved. Users are placed in a queue, and the system notifies them when reserved items become available. This feature streamlines access to high-demand resources while maintaining fairness among users.Member Management: Library administrators are provided with advanced tools to manage user accounts, and track borrowing history. These features enable staff to efficiently monitor activities and support user engagement.Automated Notifications: The system sends automated alerts to users regarding due dates, overdue materials, and availability of reserved items. This ensures timely actions by users and reduces the likelihood of delays or lost resources.Inventory and Resource Management: Technical staff can manage the addition, updating, and removal of library materials. The system helps maintain an organized inventory, supporting informed decision-making regarding resource allocation.Fines and Payments Management: Fines for overdue or damaged materials are tracked and managed automatically by the system.Reporting and Analytics: The system generates analytical reports on borrowing trends, material usage, and overdue items. These insights assist administrators in evaluating resource performance and improving library services.User-Friendly Interface: The web-based interface is intuitive, allowing users to navigate easily, search efficiently, and complete transactions independently, ensuring accessibility for all users regardless of technical proficiency.2.3 Strength of Existing SystemLow Operational Cost:  The manual system does not require computers, software, or network infrastructure, making it relatively inexpensive to maintain.Simplicity of Use: Library staff can perform tasks like book registration or lending without needing technical skills or system training.Independence from Technology: The manual system continues to function even during power outages or technical failures since it relies entirely on paper records.No Cyber security Concerns: Since there is no digital data, the system is safe from hacking, viruses, or system crashes.2.4 Weaknesses of the ExistingTime-Consuming OperationsManual processes for book registration, circulation (in-library borrowing), reservation, and return consume significant time for librarians, library admins, and students. Approval from department heads further slows down borrowing requests.Limited AccessibilityStudents must visit the library in person to check book availability, make reservations, or use materials within the library. High Risk of Data Loss or DamageWhile book registration is done using Excel, other operations such as borrowing, returning, and reservations rely on paper-based records. This combination is prone to errors, misplacement, or damage, which can result in incomplete borrowing histories, lost reservation requests, or mismanaged fines.Inefficient Book and Circulation TrackingTracking in-library circulation, borrowing status, and material availability is cumbersome. Department approvals, circulation restrictions, and multi-library management increase the complexity and risk of errors.Limited Oversight for Multi-Library ManagementWithout a centralized system, managing multiple libraries across campuses is challenging. Assigning library admins, monitoring borrowing activities, and enforcing rules for each library is difficult and inconsistent.Poor User ExperienceStudents face long queues, delays, and confusion during borrowing or circulation, especially when materials are in high demand. Lack of Real-Time InformationStudents cannot know the real-time availability of materials or the status of their borrowing or reservation requests until they physically check the library.Limited Reporting and Data AnalysisGenerating reports on book usage, circulation trends, borrowing frequency, or popular titles is difficult without a digital system. This limits the library admin’s ability to make data-driven decisions.CHAPTER THREE: SYSTEM FEATURES3.1 IntroductionThis chapter provides an SRS for the proposed system that details the necessary features, behaviors, and constraints to help in the design and development of the project. Library ManagementThe system shall allow the super admin to add new libraries to the respective campuses.The system shall allow the super admin to assign library admins to each library.3.  Physical and Digital Material ManagementPhysical MaterialsThe system shall allow technical staff to manage all physical library materials, including books, magazines, research papers, theses, and other printed items.The system shall allow technical staff to scan the barcode of a new material to register it in the system automatically, for easily retrieval, storing all relevant details.Digital Material ManagementDigital Materials include: PDFs, eBooks, journals, lecture slides, research papers, etc.The system shall allow technical staff to upload digital materials and edit digital resource metadata (e.g., title, author, description, file type, file-size, publication year).The system shall store and update the digital right protection of each digital resource (e.g., enabled, disabled).4. Physical Material Borrowing, Return, Fines and PaymentBorrowingThe system shall allow only eligible library members, as pre-approved by their respective department heads, to borrow physical library materials, including books, magazines, theses, research papers, and other available items.The system shall allow members to view a list of their currently borrowed materials along with the corresponding due dates.The system shall prevent borrowing if the member has outstanding fines exceeding a defined limit or if the member’s account is inactive or suspendedThe system shall allow borrowing returnsThe system shall allow members to return borrowed materials.Alternatively, the stack staffs shall scan the barcode of each returned item to confirm which material is being returned.The system shall update the borrowing record, mark the item as “available,” and calculate fines for overdue materials, if applicable.Fines and PaymentsThe system shall calculate fines for overdue, lost materials according to library policies.Members shall be able to view their outstanding fines.The system shall allow stack staffs to Process fines and payments.The system shall allow payments for fines using:Online or automated payment methods (e.g., Chapa payment gateway)Manual payments (processed by librarians).6.  Search and RetrievalThe system shall allow library member to search library materials (books, magazines, theses, research papers, and other physical or digital materials) by title, author, category, subject, or material type.The system shall provide filtering and sorting options to refine search results, such as by publication year, genre, material type, or availability.7.  Reservation FeaturesThe system shall handle reservation queues on a first-come, first-served basis.The system shall notify the next user in the queue when a reserved item becomes available.The system shall allow library member to reserve unavailable physical materials online.8.  NotificationsThe system shall send email or in-system notifications for:Reserved material becomes availableBorrowing confirmationDue date remindersOverdue penalties9.  CirculationThe system shall allow library members to access and read physical materials inside the library.The front desk staff shall record each in-library usage circulation, linking the material to the member’s account to track usage.3.3 Non-Functional Requirements The proposed System contains several non-functional features ensuring a secure, efficient, and user-friendly experience for all members of Hawassa University. System design is a critical phase in the software development life cycle, as it translates the requirements identified in the System Requirements Specification (SRS) into a structured and implementable solution.The design phase defines how the system will be organized, how its components will interact, and how data will be stored, processed, and accessed. It provides a clear blueprint that guides developers during implementation and ensures that the final system meets functional and non-functional requirements such as performance, security, usability, and scalability.This chapter focuses on the overall architectural structure of the system, including its major components, interactions, and design considerations. It ensures that the proposed system is robust, maintainable, and capable of supporting both web and mobile platforms while efficiently managing physical and digital library resources.4.2 Purpose of the System Design DocumentThe purpose of this System Design Document (SDD) is to provide a detailed technical description of how the proposed system will be structured and implemented based on the requirements defined in Chapter Three.Specifically, this document aims to:Translate functional and non-functional requirements into a clear system architecture.Define the logical organization of system components such as frontend, backend, database, and external services.Describe how different system modules interact to support library operations such as searching, borrowing, returning, reserving, circulation and accessing digital materials.Serve as a reference for developers, testers, and system administrators during implementation and maintenance.Ensure consistency, scalability, and maintainability of the system throughout its lifecycle.By documenting the system design in a structured manner, this chapter minimizes ambiguity, reduces development risks, and ensures that all stakeholders have a shared understanding of how the system will function technically.4.3 ScopeThe scope of this system design covers the architectural and structural design of the development of proposed system. The primary goal is to ensure data integrity and eliminate anomalies during database operations such as insertion, deletion, and updates. The process involves breaking down large, complex tables into smaller, more manageable ones, while establishing relationships between them.There are several normal forms (NF) that define the degree to which a database has been normalized. Each normal form represents a level of organization and elimination of redundancies. The commonly discussed normal forms include 1NF (First Normal Form), 2NF (Second Normal Form), and 3NF (Third Normal Form).First Normal Form (1NF)The First Normal Form (1NF) is the foundational step in database normalization, ensuring that the database is free of duplicate records and that all columns contain indivisible values. In the context of the HU-DLRBS, achieving 1NF ensures that the data is organized into structured tables with unique rows and no repeating groups. For this project, the following constraints are applied to ensure compliance with 1NF: Primary Key: Each table includes a unique identifier (primary key) that ensures no duplicate rows exist. Atomic Values: All columns in each table store atomic values, meaning each field contains a single piece of information. Same Data Type: Each column maintains consistent data types across all rows, ensuring data integrity and proper functionality.The evaluation of each table in the system against these criteria is outlined in the table below: Table NameConstraint 1: Primary KeyConstraint 2: Atomic ValuesConstraint 3: Same Data TypeUserPassPassPassMemberPassPassPassStaffPassPassPassDepartment_headPassPassPassDigital_MaterialPassFailPassPhysical_MaterialPassFailPassReservationPassPassPassBorrowingPassPassPassReturnPassPassPassPaymentPassPassPassPhysical_Material_StatsPassPassPassDigital_Material_StatsPassPassPassNotificationPassPassPassCirculationPassPassPassLibraryPassPassPassTable  SEQ Table \* ARABIC 53.1NF Checking TableNote: The columns shown in the tables below are a subset of the full table. CHAPTER FIVE: CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS5.1 ConclusionThe development and analysis of the proposed system have demonstrated the potential of digital technologies in transforming traditional library services into efficient, accessible, and user-centered platforms. The project is designed to address common challenges found in conventional libraries, such as manual record keeping, limited access to physical materials, inefficient borrowing processes, and lack of real-time availability information.Chapter one laid the foundation of the project by presenting the background of the study, problem statement, objectives, scope, and methodology. These elements clearly defined the need for a modern digital library system and guided the overall direction of the project. The feasibility analysis confirmed that the proposed system is technically achievable, economically viable, and operationally suitable for academic and institutional environments.In Chapter Two, an in-depth review of existing traditional and digital library systems was conducted. The analysis revealed key limitations, including reliance on manual processes, limited automation, poor user experience, and inadequate tracking of borrowed materials. These findings played a crucial role in shaping the proposed system by highlighting areas that required improvement and innovation.Chapter Three focused on system analysis and requirements specification. Functional requirements such as user registration, digital book browsing, borrowing and returning materials, reservation handling, and fine management were identified. Non-functional requirements, including security, usability, performance, and scalability, were also emphasized to ensure system reliability. UML diagrams were used to visually represent system behavior and interactions, improving clarity and supporting effective system design.Chapter Four presented the system design of the proposed system, detailing its architectural structure, database design, and process flows. The design ensured secure data handling, efficient transaction processing, and seamless interaction between users and the system. Emphasis was placed on maintainability, scalability, and user-friendly interfaces to support future enhancements and long-term usage.Overall, this project successfully delivers a comprehensive digital solution that improves access to library resources, simplifies borrowing operations, and enhances user experience. The HU-DLRBS not only addresses existing challenges but also sets a strong foundation for modern, technology-driven library management.5.2 RecommendationsTo enhance the effectiveness, reliability, and long-term sustainability of Digital Library Reservation and Book Borrowing System, continuous system maintenance and periodic updates are strongly recommended. Regular maintenance will help address software bugs, improve system performance, and ensure data security as user demand grows. In addition, providing proper training and awareness programs for students, librarian, and administrative staff is essential to support smooth adoption of the system and reduce dependency on manual processes. Strengthening network infrastructure within library facilities is also important to ensure uninterrupted access to system services, especially during peak usage periods.Furthermore, integrating the system with the university’s centralized campus database for student information is highly recommended. Such integration would minimize manual data entry, reduce inconsistencies in user records, and enable accurate authentication and authorization of students across different campuses. Automated data synchronization would also support efficient approval workflows and improve overall administrative coordination. To safeguard system reliability, regular data backup and recovery mechanisms should be implemented, while library policies should be continuously reviewed and aligned with system functionalities to ensure consistent enforcement of borrowing rules, fines, and access privileges.
